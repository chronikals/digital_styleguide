Using colors

Choosing background colors

Use color to segment your content

Different backgrounds can be used within the same application to group and separate content on the screen, and to establish visual hierarchy.

In some applications, the main work space is very complex and busy, and a clear distinction between different types of content is needed. For example, if your filter panel plays a secondary role in your interface, you might want to keep it as neutral as possible. If you want to bring it into focus, you could use a darker or lighter gray with higher contrast to the main background color.

Consider the context of use

Choosing a suitable background color depends a great deal on the environment your product will be used in:

  • Is it used in an office environment, or outside where strong sunlight may have an impact on legibility?
  • Is the screen mounted on a white cabinet, and should the interface have a dark background to stand out?
  • Will it be used in dark surroundings, where bright colors can disturb night vision?

Which output device?

Before deciding on a background color, take your output device(s) into account. If your application will run on a screen with poor contrast, make sure the colors you choose provide good legibility and sufficient contrast.

The blue accent color scale

The blue accent color scale is used to enhance or temporarily draw attention to specific items in the user interface. Blue should be the primary choice of color to accentuate or emphasize values, data and information, and for graphs and visualizations.

Data visualization

Data visualization colors and critical colors are used to identify, highlight and differentiate data and information. The color selection is identical both for light and dark interfaces.

Palette examples

The full range of data visualization colors is not meant to be used all at once. Consider the following when choosing a color palette for your visualizations:

  • What is the purpose of the colors?
  • Should they distinguish unrelated values/objects, or show relations? Will contrasting colors or shades of a single color best convey your point?
  • How many colors do you need?
  • Can the user select data?

Critical colors

6 critical colors are defined: Yellow, Orange, Red, Magenta, Cyan and Blue. These colors are strong with high contrast, and should only be used to attract the user’s attention to an anomaly, such as an alarm, error or important notification.

Red

Used as the primary alarm color. Red is also used for error messages, status lamps, invalid input in text fields etc.

Yellow

Often used in warning messages and on status lamps to indicate a tentative or unclear situation.

Safety critical systems

For some systems it is not possible to use these suggested critical colors due to clear conventions or industry standards. In these cases the rest of the specified colors in this style guide should still be used.

To avoid confusion and possible dangerous situations for safety critical systems, do not combine the following similar critical and data visualization colors in the same interface:

  • Magenta and Pink
  • Cyan and Turquoise

Contrast and accessibility

Always provide sufficient contrast

To accommodate the vast range of users, contexts of use, output devices and displays, an important aspect when working with colors is providing sufficient contrast between foreground and background.

The contrast ratio between a color and its background ranges from 1-21 based on its luminance, or intensity of light emitted. Contrast ratios represent how different a color is from another color, commonly written as 1:1 or 21:1. The higher the difference between the two numbers in the ratio, the greater the difference in relative luminance between the colors. For text, font size and style will also influence the contrast ratio.

We recommend always testing the legibility of the main content of your interface on actual users in a realistic environment. In general, we recommend aiming for a contrast ratio of at least 3:1 for all essential information.

There are several online tools for checking contrast ratio, for example: http://contrast-finder.tanaguru.com

Pay special attention to text on colored backgrounds

Text isn’t always against a neutral light or dark background. When setting text against a colored background, for example on a blue button, maintaining a good contrast ratio can get tricky. The contrast of both light and dark text against many vivid colors (e.g. red, green, blue, orange, etc.) may fail generic contrast tests because of the similar values in terms of luminosity. However, to the human eye, the contrast may still be perceived as sufficient and legible. In these situations – use your best judgement, make sure you test on a representative group of users, and add as much contrast as you can spare.

Don't rely on color alone

While color can be useful to convey information, a good rule of thumb is to not let color alone be the only way important information is conveyed. Also provide additional identification that does not rely on color perception, for example labels, different shapes or patterns.

Be aware of environmental factors

Finally, when selecting colors, bear in mind that environmental factors like glare or outdoor use can seriously impact contrast. It’s easier to dim a screen than struggle with gray-on-gray, so when in doubt, use more contrast rather than less.
